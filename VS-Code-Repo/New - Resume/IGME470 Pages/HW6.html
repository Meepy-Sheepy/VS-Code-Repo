Homework 6: Light Theremin

9/15/25

Making Some Noise

Today, I'm going to be working on Chapter 6: Light Theremin. If you don't know what a Theremin is, neither did I! It's apparently an instrument that makes sounds based on the movements of a musician's hands around the instrument. The Theremin detects where a performer's hands are in relation to 2 antennas by reading the capacitive change on the antennas. Kinda weird and it was made in 1928--way farther back than I thought it would be.

How this works for this project though is instead of using the movements of hands, I'm going to be using light. By using a phototransistor, I can detect amount of light that passes through the phototransistor and change the amount of light by waving my hand over it. The amount of light will determine the voltage on the analog pin, which will in turn determine what frequency note to play.

Piezo Pleasantries

Thankfully, the build didn't take too long. There were very little parts that I had to add for this project to work. A new part that I added is a piezo. A piezo is a small element that vibrates when it receives electricity. When it moves, it displaces the air around it, creating sound waves. What's cool about the piezo is that it can be either polarized or non-polarized. This one was non-polarized so I didn't need to worry about which leg was for ground or power.

To explain the BTS of the project, at the beginning it detects the light around it for 5 seconds, sensing the highs and lows of the light around it. After that, we read out the phototransistor's values, map its voltage (0 - 1023) to pitch (50 - 4000), finally we assign pitch to the piezo and then we have sound.

When I got it to work, it was very grating. Like not very pleasant to listen to. However,I changed the delay from 10 ms to 100ms and it became kinda a bop.
