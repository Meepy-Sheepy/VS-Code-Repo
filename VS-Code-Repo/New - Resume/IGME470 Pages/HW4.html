Homework 4: Color Mixing Lamp

9/10/25

Rainbows Galor

Today I'm going to be working on Chapter 4: Color Mixing Lamp. Essentially what I'm doing is manipulating what color a special LED (Common Cathode RGB LED) is going to emit. I'll be using a new technique known as Pulse Width Modulation (PWM) to fade LEDs. PWM rapidly turns the output pin high and low over a fixed period of time. So using phototransistors (components that generate a current proportional to quanitity of light absorbed), I'll be able to change the LED's color based on how much light is being absorbed by any of the phototransistors.

Before I dive right into the project, I needed to organize the resistors. Most of them were still held on by tape and once I took one out, I can't really tape it back, so I got some bags from Wegmans and put each type of resistor in their own bag with a little paper labeling what type it is. Normally, I would use green tape and label it that way, but I didn't have it so that was the next best thing. Finally, it looks so much better in the plastic organizer and it takes a lot less time figuring out which resistor is which.

The Start

This was the first time the book told me to use phototransistors and to put each leg on either side of the breadboard. It was a weird thing to tell me to do, but from my understanding, I think it's mainly used to spread the parts out evenly so it's more readable and prevent overlap. It does look more professional and cooler, so I'm going to try and keep doing that.

The Horror Of This HW

Okay...well I did finish the project but not without some nails being pulled out and my hair withering. It took such willpower to not throw my 80 dollar Arduino set out the window. So here's the tea: Basically, these phototransistors are inside these yellow bricks. The yellow bricks have slits which allows the gel to stay on and wrap around the phototransistors. This gel are in colors red, green, and blue and when one is wrapped around a phototransistor, it absorbs the color and translates it to the common cathode RBG LED. The gel, was the culprit of my anguish--it kept popping out of the slits and making my day worse. The yellow bricks also were annoying. Since the phototransistors don't keep the bricks in place, they slowly fall down and if you're fiddling with a gel, any of the yellow bricks would fall down, causing MORE gels to pop out. Terribly designed I might say.

When I did get it working though, it was very cool seeing how the phototransistors translated data to the RGB LED. It's strange to see how it's just 0's and 1's at the end of the day.

P.S. It's hard to tell in the photo, but the colors do change a lot more what is shown.

Conclusion

I'm not sure how I would use this in other projects. The setup itself is very finnicky and left much to be desired. Maybe if you wanted a lamp that changed color throughout the day, that could be a cool side project. Overall, not a great experience. Hopefully this was the worst of the worst and things can only go up from here!
