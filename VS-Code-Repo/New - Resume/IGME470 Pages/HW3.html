For The Love Of--!

Today I'm doing Chapter 3: Love-O-Meter. The general vibe of the project is to use LEDs for tempature sensing. Up until now, we've been very binary with our LEDs, either it's on or off. Now we're going to use LEDs to get a range of temperatures and depending on hot/cold the room is, none, some, or all the lights will be on.

We're gonna use a new part, the temperature sensor to detect the temperature around me, all the usual suspects of parts will also be need (i.e jumper cables resistors, etc, etc.)

It's ALIVE

Getting into the thick of it, it was a little difficult at first to see which part was the temperature sensor. It's so hard to read the label when the part is black--but I found it just from comparing the shape of the image to the part. The first picture on the right doesn't detail all the code (it was too big) so I just took a photo of the part where the LEDs change their status (on or off). Essentially, what is happening is that we compare the current temperature to the baseline temperature (with some wiggle room) and if it's below that, then all the LEDs are off using LOW. Then it compares in between temperatures, and every time it crosses a certain threshold, one more LED turns on until the temperature exceeds all the prerequisites, which then at that point, all the LEDs are on.

However, when I turned it on, the room temperature exceeded all the comparisons! I learned this afterwards, but I could've changed the baseline temperature value to match my room, but what I did instead was to ice my finger for a moment and touch the sensor to drastically change temperatures. To increase the temperature, I exhaled on it gently. I would say it worked very effectively. This is a pretty good way of debugging the temperature sensor, you get to know immediatly if it responds to the rapid change in the environment.

The Sensor Value, I'm not entirely sure what it means. It says it "stores the reading from your sensor." Sometimes in this book I wish it told me the "what" and "why" we do these things. The next one is how much power is on the pin (the math is a little strange ngl). The last one shows what temperature (in Celsius) is coming off of the sensor (the math is also peculiar here).
